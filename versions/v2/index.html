The four subsystems

How Aether turns a sealed box into a living organism

Every subsystem is designed around one principle: capture and recirculate, rather than expel. Here's what the engine above is actually modeling.

01 · ARCHITECTURAL SHELL

The passive respiratory system

A porous double-skin façade of aerogel-infused composite panels. The cavity between skins is a thermal buffer: by day it vents hot air upward via the stack effect; by night it draws cool air in to pre-cool the structure.

  • CFD dictates panel porosity — hot zones vent, cool zones seal.
  • A central hollow core acts as a thermal chimney, drawing hot aisle air up by natural convection.
  • Passive wind-catchers at the top spin as exhaust accelerates, turning heat movement into electricity.
02 · SERVER RACKS

The micro-climate cell

Each Aether-Rack is a sealed thermal unit. Coolant enters at the bottom through immersion baths or direct-to-chip micro-channels and exits at the top into a heat-exchange loop — hot exhaust never mixes with room air.

  • High-conductivity aluminum frames radiate low-grade heat as passive sinks.
  • Thermoelectric generators on heat spreaders reclaim ~5–10% of the thermal gradient as power for local sensors, fans, and lighting.
03 · HYBRID FLUID COOLING

Three loops, no dumping

Servers are submerged in non-conductive dielectric fluid — eliminating rack fans removes 10–20% of total power immediately. The heated fluid is then a resource, not exhaust.

  • District heating — piped to nearby homes for space heating and hot water.
  • Agricultural greenhouses — a symbiotic local growing ecosystem.
  • Adiabatic bypass — when it's cold out, chillers switch off entirely, saving ~90% of cooling-tower water.
04 · MICRO-GRID + AI

Generate, store, and predict

BIPV solar glass and chimney micro-wind feed the building; a thermal battery freezes water with cheap daytime power and melts it at night to shift cooling load off peak.

  • Thousands of IoT sensors build live internal heatmaps.
  • ML pre-cools specific zones ahead of scheduled high-compute tasks.
  • Idle zones drop to standby; airflow is redirected to active zones.
Façade module · structural + thermal

Inside the wall that harvests its own heat

The façade panels bear no building load — they hang from a primary steel exoskeleton and "float" in their frames to absorb thermal expansion. Each 3 m² module is a self-powered, energy-saving unit.

The whole assembly is designed for modular replacement — individual panels swap out without touching the building's integrity. The stack effect in the cavity is harvested three ways: micro-turbines powering the façade's own controls, TEGs on the exchanger coils, and a phase-change thermal battery that stores daytime heat for nighttime district heating.

⛰ The synthesis, voiced

Cooling Systems leads at 59% — it leads, but it doesn't own the story. When we look at novel combinations, circular resource flows (Recycling Systems) rise to 9%, revealing untapped potential in reusing waste heat. The colony's contrarian pick — High Efficiency — survives as the sharpest strategic insight: attacking compute inefficiency reduces the thermodynamic burden at the source, the only lever that meaningfully addresses the 20–100+ MW scale. Aether's design is the reconciliation: Sustainable Design + Optimized Cooling + Low Environmental Impact, built to leverage local resources and recycle what it would otherwise dump.

? Questions that force better design

Use these when a data center, warehouse-scale compute facility, or large commercial project appears on a local agenda.

Where does the heat go?
Ask whether waste heat will be captured for district heating, greenhouses, pre-heating water, or another nearby use.
How much water is consumed?
Ask for projected water usage, evaporative-cooling reliance, drought contingencies, and whether closed-loop or dry-cooling options were evaluated.
What is the cooling architecture?
Ask whether the design uses immersion cooling, direct-to-chip cooling, containment, free cooling, heatmaps, and predictive controls.
Does the building work with the climate?
Ask whether orientation, shading, airflow, stack effect, double-skin façades, and local weather patterns reduce mechanical load.
What happens during peak grid stress?
Ask about load shifting, thermal batteries, demand response, on-site generation, and how compute jobs are scheduled around grid conditions.
How will performance be verified?
Ask for public reporting of PUE, WUE, emissions, water withdrawals, heat reuse, and post-occupancy commissioning results.
